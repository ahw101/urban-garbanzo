Facts

The Yangtze is not just a river; it is a geographic titan that sustains one-third of the population of the world's most populous nation. Here are the core statistics and biological wonders of the "Chang Jiang."

🌍 Geographic Scale

  • Length: Stretching 6,300 kilometres (3,915 miles), it is the longest river in Asia and the third-longest in the world.
  • The Basin: It drains an area of nearly 1.8 million square kilometres, accounting for 20% of China’s total land area.
  • Economic Impact: The Yangtze River basin contributes approximately 40% of China's GDP, acting as the nation's primary industrial heartland.

🐾 Unique Biodiversity

The river is home to some of the rarest and most endangered species on the planet, many of which are found nowhere else.

  • The Yangtze Finless Porpoise: Known as the "smiling angel," this is the only freshwater porpoise in the world. Only about 1,000 remain in the wild.
  • The Chinese Alligator: One of only two alligator species in the world, this small crocodilian is critically endangered and survives in small pockets of the lower Yangtze.
  • Extinct Legends: The Baiji (Yangtze River Dolphin) was declared functionally extinct in 2006, serving as a tragic reminder of the river's environmental challenges.

🏔️ Journey from Peak to Sea

  • Source: The river begins high on the Tibetan Plateau at an elevation of over 5,000 metres.
  • Mouth: It empties into the East China Sea at Shanghai, where it creates a massive delta that grows by several meters every year due to sediment.
  • Bridges: For centuries, not a single bridge crossed the Yangtze. Today, there are over 100 bridges and tunnels, including the massive Sutong Bridge.
